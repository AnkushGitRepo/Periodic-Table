Cesium (Cs)

Atomic Number 55 | Highly Reactive Alkali Metal

Overview

Cesium is a chemical element with the symbol Cs and atomic number 55. It is a soft, golden-yellow metal that is highly reactive and one of the most electropositive and alkaline elements. Cesium is primarily used in atomic clocks and in various industrial applications.

Discovered in 1860 by Robert Bunsen and Gustav Kirchhoff using flame spectroscopy, cesium gets its name from the Latin word "caesius," meaning sky blue, due to the blue lines in its emission spectrum.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 55
  • Atomic Mass: 132.91 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Xe] 6s1
  • Group / Block: 1 (s-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 28.5 °C
  • Boiling Point: 671 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Cesium is widely used in atomic clocks, which are the most accurate timekeeping devices. It is also used in oil and gas exploration, as well as in the manufacturing of special glasses and radiation detectors. Cesium compounds are employed in medical imaging and as catalysts in chemical reactions.

Historical Perspective

Cesium was first identified in 1860 by Robert Bunsen and Gustav Kirchhoff while analyzing mineral water. Its bright blue spectral lines were unique, leading to its classification as a new element. Over the years, cesium's applications have expanded, particularly in precision timekeeping and industrial processes.

Today, cesium is an essential material in advanced technologies and scientific research.
