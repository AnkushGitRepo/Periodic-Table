Iodine (I)

Atomic Number 53 | Essential Halogen

Overview

Iodine is a chemical element with the symbol I and atomic number 53. It is a dark, lustrous solid at room temperature that sublimates into a violet gas. Iodine is essential for human health, playing a critical role in thyroid hormone production.

Discovered in 1811 by Bernard Courtois, iodine gets its name from the Greek word "iodes," meaning violet. Its distinctive color and chemical properties make it valuable in medical, industrial, and nutritional applications.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 53
  • Atomic Mass: 126.90 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d10 5s2 5p5
  • Group / Block: 17 (p-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 113.7 °C
  • Boiling Point: 184.3 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Iodine is widely used as an antiseptic in medical settings, often in the form of povidone-iodine. It is also essential in the production of iodized salt, which prevents iodine deficiency disorders. Additionally, iodine compounds are utilized in radiography contrast agents and in organic synthesis.

Historical Perspective

Iodine was discovered in 1811 by Bernard Courtois while extracting sodium and potassium compounds from seaweed ash. Its unique violet color and chemical reactivity quickly garnered scientific attention. Over the years, iodine has become a critical element in medicine and nutrition.

Today, iodine continues to play an essential role in health, industry, and environmental studies.
