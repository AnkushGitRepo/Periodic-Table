Technetium (Tc)

Atomic Number 43 | Radioactive Transition Metal

Overview

Technetium is a chemical element with the symbol Tc and atomic number 43. It is a silvery-gray transition metal and is notable for being the lightest element with no stable isotopes. All technetium isotopes are radioactive, making it unique among the lighter elements.

Discovered in 1937 by Carlo Perrier and Emilio Segrè, technetium was the first artificially produced element. Its name is derived from the Greek word "technetos," meaning artificial.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 43
  • Atomic Mass: [98] u (most stable isotope)
  • Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d5 5s2
  • Group / Block: 7 (d-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 2157 °C
  • Boiling Point: 4265 °C
  • Radioactive: Yes

Technetium is used in medical imaging, particularly in radiopharmaceuticals for diagnostic scans, such as in technetium-99m. It is also used as a corrosion inhibitor in steel and in research on radioactive decay. Due to its unique properties, technetium has limited but significant industrial and medical applications.

Historical Perspective

Technetium was first artificially produced in 1937 by Carlo Perrier and Emilio Segrè, who discovered it in a sample of molybdenum bombarded with deuterons. This discovery marked a milestone in nuclear chemistry and the understanding of isotopes.

Today, technetium is widely used in nuclear medicine and scientific research, demonstrating its significance despite its rarity and radioactivity.
