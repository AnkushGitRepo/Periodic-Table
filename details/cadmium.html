Cadmium (Cd)

Atomic Number 48 | Toxic Transition Metal

Overview

Cadmium is a chemical element with the symbol Cd and atomic number 48. It is a soft, bluish-white metal that is chemically similar to zinc and mercury. Cadmium is toxic to humans and is primarily used in batteries, pigments, and electroplating.

Discovered in 1817 by Friedrich Stromeyer, cadmium was initially used as a pigment for its bright yellow color. Today, its toxicity limits its use to specialized industrial and scientific applications.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 48
  • Atomic Mass: 112.41 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d10 5s2
  • Group / Block: 12 (d-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 321.07 °C
  • Boiling Point: 767 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Cadmium is widely used in rechargeable batteries, particularly nickel-cadmium batteries. It is also employed in pigments, coatings, and as a corrosion-resistant plating on steel. Due to its toxicity, cadmium is heavily regulated and often replaced with safer alternatives.

Historical Perspective

Cadmium was discovered in 1817 by Friedrich Stromeyer while studying zinc carbonate. Its name is derived from the Latin word "cadmia," meaning calamine, a mineral from which it was isolated. Cadmium's early uses included pigments and alloys, but its toxicity has limited its applications in modern times.

Today, cadmium is closely monitored due to its environmental and health impacts, although it remains a critical material in specific industries.
