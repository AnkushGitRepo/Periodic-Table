Lawrencium (Lr)

Atomic Number 103 | A Synthetic and Highly Radioactive Element

Overview

Lawrencium is a chemical element with the symbol Lr and atomic number 103. It is a synthetic, radioactive metal in the actinide series. Lawrencium was first synthesized in 1961 by a team of scientists at the Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory in California.

Named in honor of Ernest Lawrence, the inventor of the cyclotron, lawrencium is primarily used in scientific research.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 103
  • Atomic Mass: 262 u (most stable isotope)
  • Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f14 7s2 7p1
  • Group / Block: Actinides (f-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C (predicted)
  • Melting Point: 1627 °C (predicted)
  • Boiling Point: Unknown
  • Density: Unknown

Lawrencium is used exclusively for scientific research to study the properties of heavy elements and their isotopes. Its high radioactivity and limited production make it unsuitable for practical applications.

Lawrencium in Action

Historical Perspective

Lawrencium was first synthesized in 1961 by bombarding a californium target with boron ions. Its name honors Ernest Lawrence, whose invention of the cyclotron revolutionized nuclear science.

Today, lawrencium remains an area of interest in the field of nuclear chemistry, contributing to the study of heavy elements.
