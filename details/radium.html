Radium (Ra)

Atomic Number 88 | Highly Radioactive Alkaline Earth Metal

Overview

Radium is a chemical element with the symbol Ra and atomic number 88. It is a silvery-white radioactive metal and is the heaviest of the alkaline earth metals. Radium was discovered in 1898 by Marie and Pierre Curie while studying uranium ore.

Due to its intense radioactivity, radium glows faintly in the dark. Historically, it was used in luminous paints for watches, clocks, and instruments, but its use has been discontinued due to health risks.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 88
  • Atomic Mass: 226 u (most stable isotope)
  • Electron Configuration: [Rn] 7s2
  • Group / Block: 2 (s-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 700 °C
  • Boiling Point: 1737 °C
  • Radioactive: Yes

Radium has limited applications today due to its radioactivity. It was once used in medicine for treating cancer through radiation therapy, but safer alternatives like cobalt-60 and cesium-137 are now preferred. Radium is primarily of historical and scientific interest.

Radium in Action

Historical Perspective

Radium was discovered in 1898 by Marie and Pierre Curie as they investigated the radioactive properties of pitchblende (uranium ore). Its name is derived from the Latin word "radius," meaning ray, to reflect its intense radioactivity.

While radium was once hailed as a miracle substance, its health risks became evident as cases of radiation sickness emerged. Today, radium is a cautionary tale in the history of scientific discovery and industrial use.
