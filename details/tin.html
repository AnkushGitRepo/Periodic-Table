Tin (Sn)

Atomic Number 50 | Versatile Post-Transition Metal

Overview

Tin is a chemical element with the symbol Sn (from Latin: stannum) and atomic number 50. It is a soft, malleable, and ductile metal that has been used by humans for thousands of years. Tin is widely used in alloys, especially bronze, and as a protective coating for other metals.

Known since antiquity, tin has played a significant role in human civilization, particularly in the production of bronze. Today, it remains an essential material in electronics and packaging.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 50
  • Atomic Mass: 118.71 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Kr] 4d10 5s2 5p2
  • Group / Block: 14 (p-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 231.93 °C
  • Boiling Point: 2602 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Tin is used extensively in the production of solder, which is essential for joining electronic components. It is also used as a protective coating for steel in tin cans, providing corrosion resistance. Tin alloys, such as bronze (tin and copper), are used in sculptures, musical instruments, and bearings.

Historical Perspective

Tin has been used since ancient times, with its earliest known use dating back to the Bronze Age. Its ability to form alloys, particularly bronze, revolutionized tool and weapon making. The name "tin" is derived from Old English "tin," reflecting its historical significance.

Today, tin continues to be a vital material in modern industries, from electronics to packaging, due to its versatility and beneficial properties.
