Tantalum (Ta)

Atomic Number 73 | Corrosion-Resistant Transition Metal

Overview

Tantalum is a chemical element with the symbol Ta and atomic number 73. It is a rare, hard, blue-gray metal known for its high melting point and exceptional resistance to corrosion. Tantalum is primarily used in the electronics industry for capacitors and high-performance alloys.

Discovered in 1802 by Anders Ekeberg, tantalum was named after the mythological figure Tantalus, reflecting the element's resistance to absorbing acid, akin to Tantalus being unable to drink water in Greek mythology.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 73
  • Atomic Mass: 180.95 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Xe] 4f14 5d3 6s2
  • Group / Block: 5 (d-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 3017 °C
  • Boiling Point: 5458 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Tantalum is widely used in the production of capacitors and resistors for electronic devices such as smartphones, laptops, and cameras. Its corrosion resistance makes it ideal for medical implants and chemical processing equipment. Additionally, tantalum alloys are used in jet engines and nuclear reactors due to their strength and durability.

Historical Perspective

Tantalum was discovered in 1802 by Swedish chemist Anders Ekeberg while analyzing mineral samples. Its name reflects its unique chemical properties, as it resists absorbing acid. The element's significance grew with the rise of the electronics industry in the 20th century.

Today, tantalum is a critical material in advanced electronics, medical technologies, and high-performance engineering, highlighting its importance in modern industries.
