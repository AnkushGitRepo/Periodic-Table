Oxygen (O)

Atomic Number 8 | ~21% of Earth's Atmosphere

Overview

Oxygen is a chemical element with the symbol O and atomic number 8. It is a highly reactive nonmetal and an oxidizing agent that readily forms compounds (notably oxides) with most elements. By mass, oxygen is the third-most abundant element in the universe, after hydrogen and helium, and the most abundant element in the Earth’s crust.

Oxygen gas (O2) makes up about 21% of Earth's atmosphere and is vital for aerobic respiration in most terrestrial life forms. Another allotrope, ozone (O3), forms the protective ozone layer in the stratosphere.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 8
  • Atomic Mass: 15.999 u
  • Electron Configuration: [He] 2s2 2p4
  • Group / Block: 16 (p-block)
  • Standard State: Gas at 25 °C
  • Melting Point (O₂): −218.79 °C
  • Boiling Point (O₂): −182.96 °C
  • Radioactive: No (stable isotopes exist)

Oxygen is crucial for combustion, respiration, water composition (H2O), and is used in various industries (steelmaking, welding, rocket propulsion, healthcare, etc.). Liquid oxygen is also used in rocket fuel, providing a high-energy oxidizer.

Historical Perspective

Oxygen was independently discovered by Carl Wilhelm Scheele in 1772 and Joseph Priestley in 1774, though Priestley is often credited with its official discovery due to earlier publication. Antoine Lavoisier named the gas "oxygène," from Greek roots meaning "acid former," reflecting the then-mistaken belief that oxygen was essential to form all acids.

The realization that oxygen was crucial for combustion and respiration fundamentally changed chemistry and biology. Lavoisier’s experiments also helped disprove the phlogiston theory of fire.
