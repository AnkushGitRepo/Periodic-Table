Protactinium (Pa)

Atomic Number 91 | A Rare and Radioactive Metal

Overview

Protactinium is a chemical element with the symbol Pa and atomic number 91. It is a dense, silvery-gray metal and belongs to the actinide series. Protactinium is highly radioactive and rare, making it a material of scientific interest.

Discovered in 1913 by German chemists Kasimir Fajans and Oswald Helmuth Göhring, protactinium was named after its relationship to actinium. Its scarcity and radioactivity have limited its applications to research.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 91
  • Atomic Mass: 231.04 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Rn] 5f2 6d1 7s2
  • Group / Block: Actinides (f-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 1568 °C
  • Boiling Point: 4027 °C
  • Density: 15.37 g/cm³

Protactinium has no significant commercial applications due to its scarcity and high radioactivity. It is primarily used in scientific research to study the properties of actinides and nuclear materials.

Protactinium in Action

Historical Perspective

Protactinium was first identified in 1913 by Fajans and Göhring and later independently discovered in 1917 by Otto Hahn and Lise Meitner. Its name reflects its position as a precursor to actinium in the decay series.

Although protactinium is not widely used, its study provides valuable insights into the behavior of radioactive elements and the structure of the periodic table.
