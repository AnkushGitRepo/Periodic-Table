Nitrogen (N)

Atomic Number 7 | ~78% of Earth’s Atmosphere

Overview

Nitrogen is a chemical element with the symbol N and atomic number 7. It is a colorless, odorless, and generally inert gas at standard temperature and pressure, constituting around 78% of Earth's atmosphere. Nitrogen is a critical component of amino acids, proteins, and nucleic acids in living organisms.

Apart from its biological significance, nitrogen is also crucial in industrial processes such as the Haber-Bosch process for ammonia production, which revolutionized agriculture by enabling large-scale fertilizer manufacturing.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 7
  • Atomic Mass: 14.007 u
  • Electron Configuration: [He] 2s2 2p3
  • Group / Block: 15 (p-block)
  • Standard State: Gas at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: −210.1 °C
  • Boiling Point: −195.8 °C
  • Radioactive: No (stable isotopes exist)

Nitrogen is essential in the production of various compounds, such as nitric acid, fertilizers, explosives, and polymers. Liquid nitrogen, due to its extremely low boiling point, is widely used as a coolant in scientific and industrial settings.

Historical Perspective

Nitrogen was first recognized as a distinct substance separate from air by Scottish physician Daniel Rutherford in 1772. He demonstrated that the “residual air” after removing oxygen and carbon dioxide would not support combustion or life, thus identifying nitrogen.

Its name comes from the Greek “nitron” (a word for native soda) and “genes” (meaning “forming”), reflecting nitrogen’s presence in saltpeter (potassium nitrate). Major breakthroughs in nitrogen chemistry, such as the Haber-Bosch process in the early 20th century, have profoundly influenced human civilization through agriculture.
