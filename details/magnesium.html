Magnesium (Mg)

Atomic Number 12 | Vital Earth Metal

Overview

Magnesium is a chemical element with the symbol Mg and atomic number 12. It is a shiny gray solid and one of the alkaline earth metals. Magnesium is the eighth most abundant element in the Earth's crust and is essential for all living cells, being a key component in chlorophyll and enzymes.

Discovered in 1808 by Sir Humphry Davy, magnesium plays a significant role in biological and industrial processes. It is widely used in the production of lightweight materials and serves as an essential dietary mineral.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 12
  • Atomic Mass: 24.305 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Ne] 3s2
  • Group / Block: 2 (s-block), Alkaline Earth Metal
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 650 °C
  • Boiling Point: 1090 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Magnesium is used extensively in the production of alloys, especially with aluminum, to make lightweight and durable materials. It is a key ingredient in fireworks and flares, producing a bright white light. Magnesium compounds, such as magnesium oxide, are used in construction and medicine. In biological systems, it is essential for the synthesis of ATP and the functioning of many enzymes.

Historical Perspective

Magnesium was first isolated in 1808 by Sir Humphry Davy, who electrolyzed a mixture of magnesia and mercuric oxide. The name "magnesium" originates from the Greek region of Magnesia, where magnesium-rich minerals were first discovered.

In ancient times, magnesium compounds were used in medicine and construction. Today, magnesium is celebrated for its critical roles in both biology and modern engineering, highlighting its versatility as an element.
