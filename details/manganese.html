Manganese (Mn)

Atomic Number 25 | Essential Transition Metal

Overview

Manganese is a chemical element with the symbol Mn and atomic number 25. It is a hard, brittle metal that is essential for steel production, where it improves strength and durability. Manganese is also crucial for various biological processes, acting as a cofactor for many enzymes.

Discovered in 1774 by Carl Wilhelm Scheele, manganese is found in minerals such as pyrolusite and rhodochrosite. Its unique properties make it indispensable in metallurgy and other industries.

Properties & Uses

  • Atomic Number: 25
  • Atomic Mass: 54.938 u
  • Electron Configuration: [Ar] 3d5 4s2
  • Group / Block: 7 (d-block)
  • Standard State: Solid at 25 °C
  • Melting Point: 1246 °C
  • Boiling Point: 2061 °C
  • Radioactive: No

Manganese is primarily used in the production of steel and aluminum alloys, where it enhances strength and resistance to wear. It is also used in dry cell batteries, pigments, and as a catalyst in various chemical reactions. In biological systems, manganese is essential for bone formation, metabolism, and the functioning of antioxidant enzymes.

Historical Perspective

Manganese was first recognized as an element in 1774 by Carl Wilhelm Scheele, and it was isolated by Johan Gottlieb Gahn shortly thereafter. Its name derives from the Latin word "magnes," meaning magnet, due to its magnetic properties when alloyed with other metals.

Today, manganese is a critical material in industrial processes, from steelmaking to battery production, and its role in biological systems highlights its importance to life on Earth.
